CRS-1 (Commercial Resupply Service 1)

Rocket Name

Falcon

Launch Site

Cape Canaveral, SLC-40

Cost Per Launch

133,000,000$

Date

2012/10/8

CRS-1 was SpaceX’s first official resupply mission to the International Space Station under NASA’s Commercial Resupply Services contract. The Dragon spacecraft carried over 400 kg of supplies and experiments to the ISS and successfully returned with materials for analysis. This mission marked SpaceX as the first private company to deliver and return cargo from the ISS, setting the stage for future human and cargo flights.

SES-10

Rocket Name

Falcon 9

Launch Site

Kennedy Space Center, LC-39A

Cost Per Launch

62,000,000$

Date

2017/3/30

The SES-10 mission was a revolutionary step in rocket reusability. It was the first mission to re-fly a previously used Falcon 9 first stage, proving SpaceX’s ability to reuse boosters safely and effectively. The rocket delivered the SES-10 communications satellite for SES S.A., enhancing television and internet coverage across Latin America. The booster landed again on the drone ship “Of Course I Still Love You,” showing that reusability could dramatically reduce launch costs.

Inspiration4

Rocket Name

Falcon 9

Launch Site

Kennedy Space Center, LC-39A

Cost Per Launch

200,000,000$

Date

2021/9/15

Inspiration4 was the first all-civilian space mission, funded by billionaire Jared Isaacman. The mission carried four private citizens — none of them professional astronauts — aboard the Crew Dragon Resilience spacecraft for a three-day journey orbiting Earth at an altitude of 585 km. The mission raised over $200 million for St. Jude Children’s Research Hospital and proved that space travel is no longer limited to government astronauts but open to private citizens.

Rocket Name

Launch Site

Cape Canaveral, SLC-40

Cost Per Launch

62,000,000$

Date

2021/1/24

Transporter-1 was the first dedicated small satellite rideshare mission by SpaceX. It carried a record-breaking 143 satellites into orbit on a single Falcon 9 launch, making it the largest satellite deployment in history at the time. The mission showcased SpaceX’s rideshare program, which allows smaller companies to send satellites to space at affordable prices. It demonstrated SpaceX’s ability to provide low-cost, high-volume access to orbit, further commercializing space transportation.
